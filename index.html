Engagement Strategies by Funnel Stage
How leading brands win, activate, retain, and turn customers into advocates — and what actually doesn't work.
01 · Awareness 02 · Activation 03 · Retention 04 · Loyalty
Most companies know the funnel exists — and still do the exact same thing at every stage. Same email to someone who just found them. Same discount to someone who was never going to leave.
The question we're answering today: What does a company actually do at each stage to move the customer forward, keep them around, and turn them into someone who defends the brand?

Not in theory. With real examples. What works — and what doesn't. Because the mistakes are just as important as the wins.
01
Stage 1
Awareness
Does the customer even know I exist?
Goal: capture attention and be remembered

Awareness isn't just the brand showing up — it's the customer noticing it. And better yet, remembering it. Those are two very different things.

The most common mistake: trying to talk to everyone with the same message — and ending up talking to no one in particular.

Awareness Real-world examples
Spotify
Spotify Wrapped
  • Turns personal listening data into a shareable visual story — no paid media needed
  • Users share it voluntarily because it says something about who they are, not just what they listen to
  • Distribution cost is zero — the user becomes the channel
  • Generated massive organic reach across platforms every December
When content is personal enough, the user becomes your distribution channel.
Rappi · Colombia
Hyper-local Influencers
  • Activates neighborhood creators in Medellín and Cali — not macro-influencers in a studio
  • Shows the app in real everyday contexts: their corner, their usual restaurant
  • Cultural relevance makes the message feel personal, not like a national campaign
  • Hyper-local converts 2–3× better than mass awareness
Cultural relevance makes all the difference. People pay more attention to someone who seems to live their same life.
Duolingo
Mascot as Viral Character
  • The Duo owl started as an internet meme — Duolingo didn't create it
  • They adopted it, amplified it, and made it the official brand voice
  • Joined a conversation that already existed instead of trying to interrupt it
  • Result: global awareness with zero paid media spend
Participating in popular culture with self-awareness generates organic awareness no budget can replicate.
Awareness What works vs. what doesn't
What works
  • ✓Personalized content users want to share — identity-driven, not brand-driven
  • ✓Lookalike audience targeting based on existing best customers
  • ✓Local cultural context — not the same message for the whole country
  • ✓Joining existing conversations, not interrupting them with ads
What doesn't work
  • ✗Talking to "everyone" with one message — ends up talking to no one
  • ✗Measuring awareness only by paid impressions
  • ✗Generic content with no connection to the user's actual life
  • ✗Ignoring the user's potential role as a distribution channel
KPIs Organic reach Share of voice Brand recall Share rate Lookalike conversion rate
02
Stage 2
Activation
Does the customer try and understand the value?
Goal: the "aha moment" — the instant value becomes real

Activation is the stage where most customers are lost. The reason is always the same: the company puts its own needs before the customer's.

The principle: value comes first. Friction comes after. Registration is friction — move it to after the aha moment, not before.

Activation Real-world examples
Duolingo
Value before sign-up
  • Drops you into a real lesson before asking you to create an account
  • First exercise: under 1 minute, visual, satisfying — you feel like you learned something
  • Registration comes after the aha moment, not before
  • This shift dramatically reduces drop-off before signup
The registration didn't disappear — it just moved. That small shift changes everything.
Notion
Templates as activation
  • Instead of a blank page (intimidating), dozens of ready-to-use templates from day one
  • Click a template → 30 seconds later you have something working
  • User feels concrete productivity before understanding how the tool works
  • Avoids the classic mistake: a long tutorial that explains instead of demonstrates
Nobody wants to read how something works. Everyone wants to feel like they're already using it.
Nu (Nubank) · Latam
Gamified onboarding
  • Turned the credit card application into a game: progress bar + celebration at each step
  • Applying feels like a personal achievement, not a bureaucratic errand
  • Uses the psychological principle of progress: we want to finish what we started
  • Pulled this off in banking — historically one of the worst sectors for activation
The perception of progress triggers the impulse to complete — users finish what they've already started.
Activation What works vs. what doesn't
What works
  • ✓Show value before asking for data or registration
  • ✓Onboarding adapted to the declared profile or use case
  • ✓Visible progress — user knows where they are and what's left
  • ✓First concrete result in under 5 minutes
What doesn't work
  • ✗Sign-up + email verification + T&C before showing anything
  • ✗Long tutorials that explain instead of demonstrate
  • ✗Generic onboarding identical for all user profiles
  • ✗Asking for too much information from the start
KPIs Time to first value Activation D1 / D7 Onboarding completion rate Drop-off by step
03
Stage 3
Retention
Does the customer come back?
Goal: build habit and reduce churn

Increasing retention by just 5% can increase profits between 25% and 95%. Yet most companies spend 5× more acquiring new customers than retaining existing ones.

Key insight from HBR: not every customer deserves the same retention effort. The cost of retaining a customer must be weighed against their CLV.

Retention Real-world examples
Duolingo
Streaks & Loss Aversion
  • Daily streak system turns language learning into an automatic habit
  • Uses loss aversion: losing hurts more than gaining satisfies
  • The owl reacting when you break the streak creates real emotional discomfort
  • Users open the app just to protect the streak — even with no intention to learn
Habit-based retention is far more durable than discount-based. Discounts create customers who wait for deals; habits create users who can't leave.
Amazon Prime
The ecosystem that makes leaving hard
  • Started with free shipping → added video, music, reading, storage, Whole Foods discounts
  • Every extra service raises the switching cost of canceling
  • Canceling Prime means losing 4+ things at once — not just delivery
  • Retains with accumulated real value — not discounts or points
The right customer, retained with the right benefit stack, generates exponentially higher CLV. (HBR)
Bancolombia
Proactive Re-engagement
  • Detects behavioral signals: no transactions in 30 days, transfers to other banks
  • Triggers personalized outreach based on that customer's real financial profile
  • Not "we miss you" — but a concrete, relevant offer for that specific person
  • Acts before the customer decides to leave, not after
Once a customer starts evaluating alternatives, the cost of keeping them rises dramatically — often it's already too late.
Retention What works vs. what doesn't
What works
  • ✓Designing habits — frequent use cycles built into the product itself
  • ✓Proactive re-engagement triggered by behavioral signals, not a calendar
  • ✓Communication tailored to CLV and customer profile — not one-size-fits-all
  • ✓Raising switching costs with real accumulated value
What doesn't work
  • ✗Discounts as primary strategy — trains customers to wait for the next offer
  • ✗Reactive re-engagement — acting after the customer has already left
  • ✗Same retention effort for all segments regardless of CLV
  • ✗Generic push notifications that add no real value
KPIs Churn rate DAU / MAU ratio Retention D30 / D90 NPS CLV by segment
04
Stage 4
Loyalty
Does the customer defend and recommend me?
Goal: turn customers into active brand advocates

Kumar's key distinction: loyalty isn't that the customer buys a lot. It's that they do things beyond the transaction — recommend, defend, co-create, forgive mistakes.

A customer who buys a lot but does none of those things isn't loyal — they're convenient. And convenient customers leave when something more convenient comes along.

Loyalty Real-world examples
Starbucks
Rewards as identity ecosystem
  • Three layers: transactional (stars), ritual (personalized drink), belonging (tribe)
  • Users share their birthday gift on Instagram — not for the coffee, but for the identity
  • When someone says "Starbucks is overpriced," loyal customers respond: "but the experience is just different"
  • A loyal customer is worth 3–5× more than a transactional one with the same purchase volume
The loyal customer doesn't just buy more — they defend the brand and forgive mistakes that would drive others away. (Kumar)
Apple
Community as product
  • No points program, no loyalty discounts, no frequent-customer tier
  • Built identity instead: shared vocabulary, aesthetic values, a tribe
  • Customers don't compare iPhone vs Android on specs — they ask "am I an iPhone person?"
  • Customers tolerate premium prices because the brand is part of their self-perception
The most durable loyalty is not rational — it's identity-based. You can't buy that with points. (Kumar: relational value)
Tigo · Colombia
Programmatic Advocacy
  • Referral program built directly into the app — minimal effort to recommend
  • Both the referrer and the new customer get real, tangible benefits
  • Removes the awkwardness and friction of "telling a friend about your phone plan"
  • Advocacy doesn't happen spontaneously — the path must be designed
A good advocacy program doesn't create advocacy — it removes the friction that was blocking it from happening.
Loyalty What works vs. what doesn't
What works
  • ✓Experiential loyalty programs — not just transactional points
  • ✓Individual recognition that goes beyond purchase volume
  • ✓Facilitating advocacy — design the path, don't wait for it to happen
  • ✓Co-creation with the most engaged customers
What doesn't work
  • ✗Measuring loyalty only by purchase volume
  • ✗Points programs with no emotional or experiential value
  • ✗Treating all "frequent" customers the same regardless of engagement
  • ✗Expecting advocacy to happen spontaneously, without enabling it
KPIs NPS Referral rate Share of wallet Customer Engagement Score Advocacy actions / user
Key Takeaways
The funnel is one continuous conversation
01
Each stage needs a different strategy. What works in awareness can kill retention. What works in loyalty is useless for activation. There is no universal message or mechanic.
02
Personalization is not putting a name in an email. It's real relevance — the right message, for the right person, at the right moment, through the right channel.
03
The truly engaged customer is worth far more than any transactional metric. Don't measure them only in sales — measure them in what they do for the brand when nobody is paying them to. (Kumar)
Awareness
Make the user your distribution channel — personalization beats media spend.
Activation
Deliver the aha moment first. Every second of friction before value is a lost user.
Retention
Design habits, not discounts. Re-engage proactively — before they look elsewhere.
Loyalty
Build identity, not points. Advocacy won't happen on its own — design the path.
